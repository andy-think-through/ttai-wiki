# Let Clients Surface Pain Points
> Let clients surface pain points themselves rather than leading them to conclusions.
> Last updated: 2026-04-08

## The Principle
The insight lands harder and feels more authentic when the client arrives at it independently. As a consultant, your job is to ask good questions and listen—not to diagnose and prescribe.

## Where Discovered
**TTAI consulting:** KW Bell audit approach. Andy's strategy is to ask discovery questions, listen for where the client voices frustration, and let them articulate the problem. This builds psychological buy-in and avoids the appearance of having a pre-set solution looking for a problem.

**Also reinforced by MJM:** "Don't pretend to know my business"—a reminder that overt expertise-signaling can be off-putting if it comes before the client has had a chance to educate you.

## Where It Applies
- [[consulting]] audits and discovery calls—ask open questions, listen, and reflect back what you hear
- [[mark]] onboarding calls—let the prospect talk about their current challenges before suggesting AI-driven creative automation
- Any initial discovery conversation where trust and credibility are being established

## How It Works
1. **Ask, don't assert.** "What's your current process for managing ad creatives?" not "Your creative process is probably chaotic."
2. **Listen for emotion.** Frustration, urgency, and excitement signal real pain.
3. **Reflect it back.** "So it sounds like you're spending a lot of time on iteration—is that right?"
4. **Let them name the problem.** "What would change if you had a faster workflow?"
